Exemplar Scenario 1.1 Foundational Core

Question: A student completely combusts $2.30\text{ g}$ of pure ethanol ($\text{C}_2\text{H}_5\text{OH}_{(l)}$, $M = 46.07\text{ g}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$) beneath a copper can containing $250.0\text{ mL}$ of pure liquid water inside an uninsulated flame calorimeter. The water temperature changes from $19.5^\circ\text{C}$ to $52.0^\circ\text{C}$. Calculate the experimental molar enthalpy of combustion ($\Delta H_c$), and explain why this value differs significantly from standard literature values ($-1367\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$).

Highly Detailed Academic Breakdown

Step 1: Quantity of heat absorbed by water matrix ($q$).

$$q = m c \Delta T$$ $$m = 250.0\text{ g} \quad (\text{assuming density } \rho = 1.00\text{ g}\cdot\text{mL}^{-1})$$ $$c = 4.184\text{ J}\cdot\text{g}^{-1}\cdot\text{K}^{-1}$$ $$\Delta T = 52.0 - 19.5 = 32.5\text{ K}$$ $$q = 250.0 \times 4.184 \times 32.5 = 33995\text{ J} = 34.00\text{ kJ}$$

Step 2: Molar calculation of limiting hydrocarbon fuel.

$$n = \frac{m}{M} = \frac{2.30\text{ g}}{46.07\text{ g}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}} = 0.04992\text{ mol}$$

Step 3: Determination of Molar Enthalpy Shift ($\Delta H_c$).

$$\Delta H_c = -\frac{q}{n} = -\frac{33.995\text{ kJ}}{0.04992\text{ mol}} = -681\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1} \quad (\text{3 s.f.})$$

Step 4: Error Vector Assessment.

The calculated experimental enthalpy ($-681\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$) is significantly less exothermic than the standard value ($-1367\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$). This discrepancy stems from structural errors:

  • Heat Losses to Surroundings: Energy escaped directly to ambient air, the copper container, or the thermometer rather than entering the water.
  • Incomplete Hydrocarbon Combustion: Restricted oxygen flow around the wick generates carbon soot ($\text{C}_{(s)}$) and carbon monoxide ($\text{CO}_{(g)}$) instead of pure carbon dioxide ($\text{CO}_{2(g)}$), yielding less thermal energy per mole of fuel.
Exemplar Scenario 1.2 Intermediate Analytical

Question: Calculate the standard enthalpy change ($\Delta H^\circ_{\text{rxn}}$) for the industrial conversion of methane gas to carbon tetrachloride via chlorine gas substitution: $$\text{CH}_{4(g)} + 4\text{Cl}_{2(g)} \rightarrow \text{CCl}_{4(g)} + 4\text{HCl}_{2(g)}$$ Using the following average gas-phase bond enthalpy parameters provided in the database matrix:

Bond VectorMean Dissociation Energy ($\text{kJ}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$)
$\text{C}-\text{H}$414
$\text{Cl}-\text{Cl}$243
$\text{C}-\text{Cl}$339
$\text{H}-\text{Cl}$431
Highly Detailed Academic Breakdown $$\Delta H = \sum \text{Bond Energies}_{\text{(Bonds Broken)}} - \sum \text{Bond Energies}_{\text{(Bonds Formed)}}$$

1. Energy needed to break reactant bonds:

  • $4 \times (\text{C}-\text{H}) = 4 \times 414 = 1656\text{ kJ}$
  • $4 \times (\text{Cl}-\text{Cl}) = 4 \times 243 = 972\text{ kJ}$
  • $\sum \text{Broken} = 1656 + 972 = +2628\text{ kJ}$

2. Energy released by forming product bonds:

  • $4 \times (\text{C}-\text{Cl}) = 4 \times 339 = 1356\text{ kJ}$
  • $4 \times (\text{H}-\text{Cl}) = 4 \times 431 = 1724\text{ kJ}$
  • $\sum \text{Formed} = 1356 + 1724 = 3080\text{ kJ}$

3. Final Balance Calculation:

$$\Delta H^\circ_{\text{rxn}} = 2628 - 3080 = -452\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$$

Since forming the new bonds releases more energy than what was needed to break the original ones, the reaction is net exothermic.

Exemplar Scenario 1.3 Advanced Mastery Tiers

Question: Calculate the standard enthalpy of formation ($\Delta H_f^\circ$) for solid anhydrous aluminium chloride ($\text{Al}_2\text{Cl}_{6(s)}$) given the following thermochemical data suite: $$\text{(1) } 2\text{Al}_{(s)} + 6\text{HCl}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Al}_2\text{Cl}_{6(aq)} + 3\text{H}_{2(g)} \quad \Delta H_1 = -1003.0\text{ kJ}$$ $$\text{(2) } \text{H}_{2(g)} + \text{Cl}_{2(g)} \rightarrow 2\text{HCl}_{(g)} \quad \Delta H_2 = -184.1\text{ kJ}$$ $$\text{(3) } \text{HCl}_{(g)} \rightarrow \text{HCl}_{(aq)} \quad \Delta H_3 = -73.2\text{ kJ}$$ $$\text{(4) } \text{Al}_2\text{Cl}_{6(s)} \rightarrow \text{Al}_2\text{Cl}_{6(aq)} \quad \Delta H_4 = -645.5\text{ kJ}$$

Highly Detailed Academic Breakdown

The target reaction is the formation equation for one mole of the compound from its constituent elements in their reference standard states:

$$2\text{Al}_{(s)} + 3\text{Cl}_{2(g)} \rightarrow \text{Al}_2\text{Cl}_{6(s)}$$

We apply Hess's Law by adjusting and adding the provided intermediate steps:

  • Keep equation (1) as written to place $2\text{Al}_{(s)}$ on the reactant side:
    $$\Delta H_A = \Delta H_1 = -1003.0\text{ kJ}$$
  • Multiply equation (2) by 3 to provide $3\text{Cl}_{2(g)}$ reactants:
    $$3\text{H}_{2(g)} + 3\text{Cl}_{2(g)} \rightarrow 6\text{HCl}_{(g)} \quad \Delta H_B = 3 \times (-184.1) = -552.3\text{ kJ}$$
  • Multiply equation (3) by 6 to match the $6\text{HCl}_{(g)}$ intermediate molecules:
    $6\text{HCl}_{(g)} \rightarrow 6\text{HCl}_{(aq)} \quad \Delta H_C = 6 \times (-73.2) = -439.2\text{ kJ}$
  • Reverse equation (4) to isolate solid $\text{Al}_2\text{Cl}_{6(s)}$ on the product side:
    $$\text{Al}_2\text{Cl}_{6(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Al}_2\text{Cl}_{6(s)} \quad \Delta H_D = -\Delta H_4 = +645.5\text{ kJ}$$

Summing all adjusted reactions gives the target equation, cancelling intermediate molecules out ($\text{HCl}_{(g)}$, $\text{HCl}_{(aq)}$, $\text{H}_{2(g)}$, and $\text{Al}_2\text{Cl}_{6(aq)}$):

$$\Delta H_f^\circ = \Delta H_A + \Delta H_B + \Delta H_C + \Delta H_D$$ $$\Delta H_f^\circ = (-1003.0) + (-552.3) + (-439.2) + 645.5 = -1349.0\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$$
Exemplar Scenario 1.4 Advanced Mastery Tiers

Question: For the industrial vaporisation of liquid bromine ($\text{Br}_{2(l)} \rightarrow \text{Br}_{2(g)}$), standard transition constants are given as $\Delta H^\circ = +31.0\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$ and $\Delta S^\circ = +93.0\text{ J}\cdot\text{K}^{-1}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$. Calculate the thermodynamic transition temperature threshold above which this process becomes spontaneous.

Highly Detailed Academic Breakdown

A reaction transitions to thermodynamic spontaneity at the threshold boundary where Gibbs Free Energy is exactly zero:

$$\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S = 0 \implies \Delta H = T\Delta S$$

CRITICAL UNIT CONVERSION STEP: Convert entropy $\Delta S^\circ$ into kilojoules ($\text{kJ}$):

$$\Delta S^\circ = \frac{93.0}{1000} = 0.0930\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{K}^{-1}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$$

Isolate and solve for the absolute temperature boundary ($T$):

$$T = \frac{\Delta H^\circ}{\Delta S^\circ} = \frac{31.0\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}}{0.0930\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{K}^{-1}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}} = 333.33\text{ K}$$

Convert this absolute thermodynamic threshold to degrees Celsius:

$$T(^\circ\text{C}) = 333.33 - 273.15 = 60.18^\circ\text{C}$$

Because the entropy term is positive ($+T\Delta S$), high temperatures increase the magnitude of the $-T\Delta S$ subtraction, making $\Delta G$ increasingly negative. Therefore, vaporisation is spontaneous at temperatures strictly exceeding $60.18^\circ\text{C}$.

Question 1Foundational Core

Define 'enthalpy' ($H$) and state how it relates to the First Law of Thermodynamics during chemical transitions.

Exemplar Solution

Enthalpy ($H$) is a thermodynamic state function that represents the total internal energy of a chemical system at constant pressure. While absolute internal energy cannot be measured directly, the enthalpy change ($\Delta H$) represents the heat exchanged with the surroundings at constant pressure. This complies with the First Law of Thermodynamics (Energy Conservation), as any change in internal chemical potential energy is balanced by an equal and opposite exchange of thermal energy across the system boundary.

Question 2 [Module 2 Bridge]Intermediate Analytical

A piece of pure iron metal ($m = 55.85\text{ g}$) absorbs $450.0\text{ J}$ of heat energy, causing its temperature to rise by $18.0\text{ K}$. Calculate the specific heat capacity ($c$) of the iron sample.

Exemplar Solution

Using the calorimetry formula: $q = m c \Delta T$. Rearranging to solve for the specific heat capacity ($c$):

$$c = \frac{q}{m \Delta T} = \frac{450.0\text{ J}}{55.85\text{ g} \times 18.0\text{ K}} = 0.448\text{ J}\cdot\text{g}^{-1}\cdot\text{K}^{-1}$$
Question 3Intermediate Analytical

Explain why the breaking of chemical bonds is always an endothermic process on a sub-microscopic level.

Exemplar Solution

Chemical bonds form because shared electrons or electrostatic forces hold atomic nuclei in a stable, lower potential energy state. To break a bond, energy must be added from the surroundings to overcome these attractive forces and pull the atoms apart. Since this step absorbs energy, bond breaking is always an endothermic process ($\Delta H > 0$).

Question 4Foundational Core

Predict the sign of the entropy change ($\Delta S$) when solid sodium chloride dissolves in water, and justify your prediction.

Exemplar Solution

The entropy change is positive ($\Delta S > 0$). Dissolving breaks down the highly structured, rigid crystal lattice of solid $\text{NaCl}$. The individual $\text{Na}^+$ and $\text{Cl}^-$ ions are freed and spread throughout the solvent, increasing the system's randomness, disorder, and total available microstates.

Question 5Advanced Mastery Tiers

State Hess's Law and explain why it remains valid regardless of the number of intermediate stages in a reaction.

Exemplar Solution

Hess's Law states that the total enthalpy change for a chemical reaction is constant, regardless of whether the reaction occurs in a single step or a series of steps. This holds true because enthalpy ($H$) is a state function. Its value depends only on the initial thermodynamic state of the reactants and the final state of the products, completely independent of the intermediate pathway taken.

Question 6 [Module 2 Bridge]Intermediate Analytical

Given that the molar enthalpy of combustion for propane is $-2220\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$, calculate the mass of propane ($\text{C}_3\text{H}_{8(g)}$, $M = 44.1\text{ g}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$) needed to release $5000\text{ kJ}$ of heat energy.

Exemplar Solution

First, find the moles of propane required to release the target amount of energy:

$$n = \frac{\text{Energy Required}}{|\Delta H_c|} = \frac{5000\text{ kJ}}{2220\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}} = 2.252\text{ mol}$$

Next, convert moles to mass using molar mass:

$$m = n \times M = 2.252\text{ mol} \times 44.1\text{ g}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1} = 99.3\text{ g} \quad (\text{3 s.f.})$$
Question 7Intermediate Analytical

A reaction is endothermic with a negative entropy change ($\Delta H > 0, \Delta S < 0$). Discuss the spontaneity of this reaction as temperature changes.

Exemplar Solution

Using the Gibbs Free Energy expression: $\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S$. Since $\Delta H$ is positive and $\Delta S$ is negative, the term $-T\Delta S$ becomes positive. Combining a positive $\Delta H$ with a positive $-T\Delta S$ term ensures that $\Delta G$ will be positive ($\Delta G > 0$) at all temperatures. Therefore, the reaction is non-spontaneous at all temperatures.

Question 8Foundational Core

Write the balanced chemical equation for the standard molar enthalpy of formation of liquid water ($\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$), including all phase states.

Exemplar Solution

The standard formation equation must generate exactly one mole of the product from its component elements in their standard stable reference states:

$$\text{H}_{2(g)} + \frac{1}{2}\text{O}_{2(g)} \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$
Question 9Intermediate Analytical

Compare how cellular respiration and photosynthesis differ in terms of their net enthalpy changes ($\Delta H$).

Exemplar Solution

Photosynthesis is an endothermic pathway ($\Delta H = +2803\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$) that stores solar energy within the chemical bonds of glucose. In contrast, cellular respiration is an exothermic pathway ($\Delta H = -2803\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$) that breaks down glucose molecules to release this stored energy for metabolic use. Because they are exact thermodynamic reverses of one another, they have identical magnitudes but opposite signs.

Question 10 [Module 3 Bridge]Advanced Mastery Tiers

A student mixes $50.0\text{ mL}$ of $1.0\text{ M }\text{HCl}_{(aq)}$ with $50.0\text{ mL}$ of $1.0\text{ M }\text{NaOH}_{(aq)}$ inside a calorimeter. The temperature rises by $6.80^\circ\text{C}$. Calculate the molar enthalpy of neutralisation ($\Delta H_{\text{neut}}$) in $\text{kJ}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$.

Exemplar Solution

Find the heat absorbed by the solution matrix ($m = 50.0 + 50.0 = 100.0\text{ g}$):

$$q = m c \Delta T = 100.0\text{ g} \times 4.184\text{ J}\cdot\text{g}^{-1}\cdot\text{K}^{-1} \times 6.80\text{ K} = 2845.12\text{ J} = 2.845\text{ kJ}$$

Calculate the moles of water formed during the neutralisation reaction:

$$n(\text{H}^+) = C \times V = 1.0\text{ M} \times 0.0500\text{ L} = 0.0500\text{ mol}$$

Calculate the molar enthalpy of neutralisation:

$$\Delta H_{\text{neut}} = -\frac{q}{n} = -\frac{2.845\text{ kJ}}{0.0500\text{ mol}} = -56.9\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$$
Question 11Intermediate Analytical

Explain why an increasing temperature makes reactions with positive enthalpy and positive entropy ($\Delta H > 0, \Delta S > 0$) more spontaneous.

Exemplar Solution

In the Gibbs Free Energy equation ($\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S$), a positive $\Delta S$ means the $-T\Delta S$ term subtracts from the overall value. As the temperature ($T$) increases, the magnitude of this negative $-T\Delta S$ term grows. Once $T\Delta S$ becomes larger than the unfavorable positive $\Delta H$, the net Gibbs Free Energy becomes negative ($\Delta G < 0$), making the reaction thermodynamically spontaneous.

Exemplar Solution

To convert Joules to Kilojoules, divide the given entropy value by exactly 1000:

$$\Delta S^\circ = \frac{145}{1000} = 0.145\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{K}^{-1}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$$
Question 13Intermediate Analytical

Predict whether the reaction $\text{CaCO}_{3(s)} \rightarrow \text{CaO}_{(s)} + \text{CO}_{2(g)}$ results in an increase or decrease in entropy, and explain why.

Exemplar Solution

The reaction results in an increase in entropy ($\Delta S > 0$). This change occurs because a highly structured solid reactant decomposes to produce a mole of carbon dioxide gas ($\text{CO}_{2(g)}$). Gaseous particles are far more disordered and have a significantly higher number of accessible microstates than solid crystal structures.

Question 14Advanced Mastery Tiers

Explain the difference between a system that is thermodynamically spontaneous and one that is kinetically rapid. Give an example of a process that is spontaneous but proceeds at an unnoticeable rate.

Exemplar Solution

Thermodynamic spontaneity indicates whether a reaction can proceed without a continuous input of external energy, which is determined solely by a negative Gibbs Free Energy ($\Delta G < 0$). It does not provide information about the reaction rate. Kinetic speed depends on the activation energy barrier ($E_a$).

An example is the conversion of diamond into graphite. This process is thermodynamically spontaneous ($\Delta G < 0$), but its exceptionally high activation energy barrier means it occurs at an unnoticeable rate under standard conditions.

Question 16 [Module 2 Bridge]Intermediate Analytical

Calculate the standard enthalpy change ($\Delta H^\circ$) for the reaction $2\text{CO}_{(g)} + \text{O}_{2(g)} \rightarrow 2\text{CO}_{2(g)}$ using standard enthalpies of formation: $\Delta H_f^\circ(\text{CO}_{(g)}) = -110.5\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$ and $\Delta H_f^\circ(\text{CO}_{2(g)}) = -393.5\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$.

Exemplar Solution $$\Delta H^\circ = \sum n \Delta H_f^\circ(\text{products}) - \sum m \Delta H_f^\circ(\text{reactants})$$ $$\Delta H^\circ = [2 \times (-393.5)] - [2 \times (-110.5) + 0]$$ $$\Delta H^\circ = [-787.0] - [-221.0] = -566.0\text{ kJ}$$
Question 17Foundational Core

State the conditions required for a system to be considered at standard state during thermodynamic evaluations.

Exemplar Solution

Standard thermodynamic states are defined by the following parameters:

  • A uniform gas pressure of exactly $100\text{ kPa}$ (or $1\text{ bar}$).
  • A solute concentration of exactly $1.0\text{ M}$ ($1.0\text{ mol}\cdot\text{L}^{-1}$) for all aqueous solutions.
  • Pure substances in their most stable physical states at a specified reference temperature, which is typically $298.15\text{ K}$ ($25^\circ\text{C}$).
Question 18Advanced Mastery Tiers

A reaction has $\Delta H^\circ = -198.2\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$ and $\Delta S^\circ = -187.3\text{ J}\cdot\text{K}^{-1}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$. Calculate the absolute value of $\Delta G^\circ$ at $25^\circ\text{C}$, and state whether the process is spontaneous.

Exemplar Solution

Convert temperature to Kelvin: $T = 25 + 273.15 = 298.15\text{ K}$.

Convert entropy to kilojoules: $\Delta S^\circ = -0.1873\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{K}^{-1}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$.

Apply the Gibbs Free Energy equation:

$$\Delta G^\circ = \Delta H^\circ - T\Delta S^\circ$$ $$\Delta G^\circ = -198.2 - [298.15 \times (-0.1873)]$$ $$\Delta G^\circ = -198.2 - [-55.843] = -142.4\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$$

Since $\Delta G^\circ$ is negative ($-142.4\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$), the process is spontaneous under standard conditions.

Question 19 [Module 1 Bridge]Intermediate Analytical

Explain how changing the physical state of water from liquid to gas ($\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)} \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(g)}$) alters both the interparticle bonding networks and the entropy of the system.

Exemplar Solution

Phase transitions are physical changes that follow thermodynamic rules. Vaporising water requires heat input to break the intermolecular hydrogen bonds holding liquid water molecules together. Breaking these bonds allows the molecules to break free into the gas phase, where they move rapidly and independently. This change dramatically increases the system's disorder and available microstates, resulting in a large positive increase in entropy ($\Delta S \gg 0$).

Question 20Advanced Mastery Tiers

An incomplete cell notation is written as: $\text{Al}_{(s)} \;\big|\; \text{Al}^{3+}_{(aq)} \;\big\|\; \text{Cu}^{2+}_{(aq)} \;\big|\; \text{Cu}_{(s)}$. Describe the composition of the salt bridge required to complete this functional galvanic cell, and explain how it maintains electrical neutrality during operation.

Exemplar Solution

The salt bridge should be saturated with an unreactive electrolyte solution, such as potassium nitrate ($\text{KNO}_3$). It completes the electrical circuit and maintains charge neutrality in each half-cell through ion migration:

  • As $\text{Al}_{(s)}$ oxidises into $\text{Al}^{3+}$ ions at the anode, negative $\text{NO}_3^-$ anions migrate from the bridge into the anolyte to balance the accumulating positive charge.
  • As $\text{Cu}^{2+}$ ions reduce into solid copper at the cathode, positive $\text{K}^+$ cations migrate from the bridge into the catholyte to replace the consumed positive charge.
