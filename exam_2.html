Question 1 (4 Marks)Module 1 Core Analysis

The following multi-line scatter plot displays the experimental periodic variations in atomic radius versus first ionisation energy for elements across Period 2 and Period 3. Analyze the explicit inverse relationship illustrated, identifying and validating any visual anomalies using core quantum and subshell mechanics.

Exemplar Analytical Solution

Across Period 2, as atomic number increases, core nuclear charge concurrently rises due to the addition of protons, while shielding remains relatively constant as electrons populate the same principal energy level shell ($n=2$). This systematically intensifies the effective nuclear charge ($Z_{\text{eff}}$), drawing the valence shell structurally closer to the nucleus and lowering the atomic radius (as shown by the descending red line).

Because the valence boundary is pulled tighter, the electrostatic pull on outer electrons is maximized, raising the energy cost to remove an electron (the rising green line). The prominent dips at Beryllium-to-Boron and Nitrogen-to-Oxygen represent subshell anomalies: Boron addresses a higher-energy $2p^1$ orbital outside a stable full $2s^2$ shield, while Oxygen experiences electron pairing spin-repulsion within its first populated $2p$ subshell position, reducing structural containment energy requirements.

Module 2 Quantitative Graphing: Plot the given gas volume metrics versus temperature readings to identify the absolute zero intercept using Charles's Law ($V \propto T$).

Data Matrix: (Point A: $-100^\circ\text{C}, 1.41\text{ L}$), (Point B: $0^\circ\text{C}, 2.23\text{ L}$), (Point C: $100^\circ\text{C}, 3.05\text{ L}$).

💡 Click precisely inside the grid to plot your coordinates. Two mapped points automatically generate your dotted line of best fit.

Points Plotted: 0/3
Exemplar Analytical Solution

Mathematical Derivation: Using linear regression formula lines or basic coordinate slopes ($y = mx + c$):

$$m = \frac{V_2 - V_1}{T_2 - T_1} = \frac{3.05 - 2.23}{100 - 0} = \frac{0.82}{100} = 0.0082\text{ L/}^\circ\text{C}$$

Setting volume $V = 0$ to find the temperature x-intercept:

$$0 = 0.0082(T) + 2.23 \implies T = \frac{-2.23}{0.0082} = \mathbf{-271.95^\circ\text{C}}$$

This experimental intercept directly validates absolute zero ($\approx -273.15^\circ\text{C}$), demonstrating that ideal gas volume drops to zero when thermal kinetic motion stops.

Question 3 (4 Marks)Advanced Quantitative Parse

Module 2 & 4 Integrated Matrix: A saturated aqueous solution containing exactly $150\text{ g}$ of an unknown salt matrix is cooled slowly from $80^\circ\text{C}$ down to $20^\circ\text{C}$ using the experimental solubility metrics below. Calculate the precise mass of solid solute that precipitates out of the system environment.

Exemplar Analytical Solution

Step 1: Extract solubility limits from graph coordinates:
- At $80^\circ\text{C}$, solubility $= 80\text{ g}$ salt per $100\text{ g }\text{H}_2\text{O}$.
- At $20^\circ\text{C}$, solubility $= 25\text{ g}$ salt per $100\text{ g }\text{H}_2\text{O}$.

Step 2: Scale parameters relative to the experimental system mass ($150\text{ g}$ mass):
The problem specifies we have $150\text{ g}$ of total saturated solution at $80^\circ\text{C}$.
A standard saturated unit consists of $80\text{ g salt} + 100\text{ g water} = 180\text{ g solution}$.
Using ratio matrices to determine actual water mass ($m_{\text{water}}$):

$$\frac{100\text{ g H}_2\text{O}}{180\text{ g solution}} = \frac{m_{\text{water}}}{150\text{ g solution}} \implies m_{\text{water}} = \frac{100 \times 150}{180} = 83.33\text{ g H}_2\text{O}$$ $$\text{Actual mass of salt initially present} = 150 - 83.33 = 66.67\text{ g salt}$$

Step 3: Evaluate maximum saturation mass retention at $20^\circ\text{C}$ over remaining water:

$$\text{Max salt retained} = 83.33\text{ g H}_2\text{O} \times \left(\frac{25\text{ g salt}}{100\text{ g H}_2\text{O}}\right) = 20.83\text{ g salt}$$ $$\text{Precipitated mass yield} = 66.67\text{ g} - 20.83\text{ g} = \mathbf{45.84\text{ g}}\text{ (or 45.8g to 3 s.f.)}$$

Question 5 (4 Marks)Advanced Kinetic Evaluation

Module 3 Kinetical Trajectory Parsing: The graph below demonstrates the potential energy progression coordinates for a reversible pathway. Derive the precise numerical value for the activation energy ($E_a$) of the reverse reaction and establish whether the forward progression path is exothermic or endothermic.

Exemplar Analytical Solution

Step-by-step Systemic Derivation:
1. Identify energy base levels from the graph layout:
- Potential Energy of Reactants ($H_{\text{reactants}}$) $= 150\text{ kJ/mol}$
- Potential Energy of Transition State Peak ($H_{\text{peak}}$) $= 250\text{ kJ/mol}$
- Potential Energy of Products ($H_{\text{products}}$) $= 50\text{ kJ/mol}$

2. The reverse reaction begins at the product baseline ($50\text{ kJ/mol}$) and must scale up to touch the localized transition state energetic peak configuration ($250\text{ kJ/mol}$):

$$E_{a\text{(reverse)}} = H_{\text{peak}} - H_{\text{products}} = 250\text{ kJ/mol} - 50\text{ kJ/mol} = \mathbf{+200\text{ kJ/mol}}$$

3. Forward path thermodynamic categorization:

$$\Delta H_{\text{forward}} = H_{\text{products}} - H_{\text{reactants}} = 50 - 150 = \mathbf{-100\text{ kJ/mol}}$$

Since the net enthalpy value is negative ($\Delta H < 0$), the forward reaction sequence is definitively exothermic.

Module 4 Statistical Mechanics Graphing: You are provided with a baseline Maxwell-Boltzmann molecular velocity profile at $298\text{ K}$ ($T_1$). Plot the modified kinetic profile curve representing the system configuration when heated to $500\text{ K}$ ($T_2$).

💡 Click inside the canvas to plot the localized curve peak maximum and the corresponding high-energy tail distribution values for the heated profile.

Exemplar Analytical Solution

When system temperature increases to $500\text{ K}$, the average kinetic energy of the population rises, causing the velocity profile peak to flatten downward and shift to the right. Because the total number of particles within the system remains constant, the area under both curves must remain identical.

The key effect is that the high-energy trailing edge of the curve flattens significantly above the fixed Activation Energy threshold ($E_a$). This creates a large increase in the fraction of molecules that meet or exceed the activation threshold ($E_k \ge E_a$), which exponentially increases the frequency of successful collisions and accelerates the net reaction rate.

Module 3 Kinetic Rate Analysis: The mass tracking line below displays the loss of mass from a flask during a standard carbonate-acid decomposition reaction over time. Calculate the instantaneous rate of reaction at exactly $t = 30\text{ seconds}$ by evaluating the geometric gradient vector.

Exemplar Analytical Solution

Geometric Tangent Resolution Method:
To determine the instantaneous rate of a curved tracking profile, evaluate the slope of the tangent line drawn at that point ($t = 30\text{ s}$).
Extract two coordinate sets from the linear dashed tangent line vector:
- Intercept 1 ($t_1 = 0\text{ s}$, $\text{Mass}_1 = 200.0\text{ g}$)
- Intercept 2 ($t_2 = 250\text{ s}$, $\text{Mass}_2 = 150.0\text{ g}$)

$$\text{Gradient (Slope)} = \frac{\text{Mass}_2 - \text{Mass}_1}{t_2 - t_1} = \frac{150.0 - 200.0}{250 - 0} = \frac{-50.0\text{ g}}{250\text{ s}} = \mathbf{-0.20\text{ g/s}}$$

The reaction rate represents the absolute value of system mass loss per unit time, resulting in an instantaneous evolution rate of $0.20\text{ g/s}$ at the $30\text{-second}$ mark.

Question 12 (4 Marks)Module 2 Quantitative Parse

Module 2 Conductometric Titration: Explain the structural changes in electrical conductivity shown in the graph below during the titration of a strong acid with a strong base.

Exemplar Analytical Solution

The initial steep drop in conductivity is driven by the neutralization of highly mobile hydronium ions ($\text{H}^+$), which react with added hydroxide ions ($\text{OH}^-$) to produce neutral water molecules ($\text{H}_2\text{O}$). These fast charge carriers are replaced in solution by less mobile salt cations ($\text{Na}^+$), lowering the overall electrical conductivity of the system.

At the equivalence point, conductivity reaches its minimum because the solution contains only neutral salt ions. Past this point, continuing to add strong base introduces excess, highly mobile hydroxide ions ($\text{OH}^-$), causing the conductivity line to rise again.

Question 14 (4 Marks)Module 4 Intermolecular Parsing

Module 1 & 4 Intermolecular Vapor Transitions: The following graph illustrates the vapor pressure curves of Ethanol versus Diethyl Ether. Identify which curve corresponds to Ethanol and justify your choice using intermolecular hydrogen-bonding dynamics.

Exemplar Analytical Solution

Curve B corresponds to Ethanol. Ethanol molecules contain a highly polar hydroxyl group ($\text{-OH}$), which forms strong, permanent network architectures of hydrogen bonds. Diethyl ether is a symmetrical molecule that cannot form mutual hydrogen bonds, relying instead on weaker dipole-dipole and dispersion forces.

Because Ethanol is held together by stronger intermolecular forces, it requires significantly higher thermal energy to overcome these attractions and escape into the gas phase. This gives Ethanol a lower vapor pressure at any given temperature compared to diethyl ether, as shown by Curve B's shifted position to the right.

Question 15 (5 Marks)Final Legend Tier Assessment

Module 4 Comprehensive Capstone: The graph below tracks Gibbs Free Energy ($\Delta G$) as a function of temperature for an industrial chemical process. Deducing your answers directly from the slope and intercept features, calculate the exact values for the system's reaction enthalpy ($\Delta H^\circ$) and entropy change ($\Delta S^\circ$).

Exemplar Analytical Solution

Step-by-step Thermodynamic Extraction:
The linear change in Gibbs Free Energy follows the standard equation $\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S$. This can be written in slope-intercept form ($y = mx + b$), where $y = \Delta G$ and $x = T$:

$$\Delta G = (-\Delta S)T + \Delta H$$

1. The y-intercept occurs where temperature $T = 0\text{ K}$, meaning the intercept directly provides the reaction enthalpy value ($\Delta H^\circ$):

$$\Delta H^\circ = \mathbf{+120\text{ kJ/mol}}$$

2. The x-intercept marks the spontaneity threshold where $\Delta G = 0$. Given this crossover occurs at $T = 400\text{ K}$:

$$0 = \Delta H - T\Delta S \implies \Delta S^\circ = \frac{\Delta H^\circ}{T}$$ $$\Delta S^\circ = \frac{+120\text{ kJ/mol}}{400\text{ K}} = +0.30\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{Mol}^{-1}\cdot\text{K}^{-1} = \mathbf{+300\text{ J}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}\cdot\text{K}^{-1}}$$

Because the slope ($m = -\Delta S$) is negative, the reaction entropy change ($\Delta S^\circ$) is positive, which drives the process toward spontaneity at higher temperatures.
