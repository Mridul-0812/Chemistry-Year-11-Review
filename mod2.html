Inquiry Question 1: What happens in chemical reactions?

Analysing macroscopic signatures, balancing laws, and stoichiometric reaction mechanics.

1. Signs of a Chemical Reaction

A chemical reaction involves the breaking and reforming of intramolecular bonds to rearrange reactant atoms into entirely new products. This is fundamentally distinct from a physical phase transition. Five primary macroscopic indicators verify a chemical change:

  • Spontaneous Temperature Deviation: Exothermic energy release (heating) or endothermic energy absorption (cooling) without external interference.
  • Evolution of a Gas: Effervescence or bubbling observed as gaseous products exit condensed phases.
  • Precipitation Formation: An insoluble solid grid settling out from the mixing of clear aqueous solutions.
  • Permanent Color Alteration: Transition in light absorption profiles due to alterations in chemical valence configurations.
  • Emission of Light/Odour: Photonic release from highly energetic transition states or the production of novel volatile compounds.

2. The Law of Conservation of Mass

The Law of Conservation of Mass states: Matter cannot be created or destroyed in an isolated chemical system. The total mass of the reactants must exactly equal the total mass of the products generated.

On an atomic level, every single atom present in the reactant phase must be accounted for within the product phase. This provides the mathematical justification for balancing chemical equations using whole-number stoichiometric coefficients.

3. Empirical Equations Across Core Reaction Classes

To master NESA assessment scenarios, you must be capable of translating word problems into balanced chemical formulas containing correct states of matter ($\text{s}$, $\text{l}$, $\text{g}$, $\text{aq}$). Below are the core diagnostic templates:

A. Metal + Oxygen $\rightarrow$ Metal Oxide

$$2\text{Mg}_{(s)} + \text{O}_{2(g)} \rightarrow 2\text{MgO}_{(s)}$$

B. Thermal Decomposition of a Metal Carbonate

$$\text{CuCO}_{3(s)} \xrightarrow{\Delta} \text{CuO}_{(s)} + \text{CO}_{2(g)}$$

C. Acid + Metal $\rightarrow$ Hydrogen Gas + Ionic Salt

$$\text{Mg}_{(s)} + 2\text{HCl}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{MgCl}_{2(aq)} + \text{H}_{2(g)}$$

D. Acid + Base $\rightarrow$ Salt + Water (Neutralisation)

$$\text{H}_2\text{SO}_{4(aq)} + 2\text{NaOH}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Na}_2\text{SO}_{4(aq)} + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

E. Acid + Metal Carbonate $\rightarrow$ Carbon Dioxide + Water + Ionic Salt

$$2\text{HNO}_{3(aq)} + \text{CaCO}_{3(s)} \rightarrow \text{Ca(NO}_3)_{2(aq)} + \text{CO}_{2(g)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

F. Precipitation Reaction (Double Displacement)

$$\text{AgNO}_{3(aq)} + \text{NaCl}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{AgCl}_{(s)} \downarrow + \text{NaNO}_{3(aq)}$$

G. Complete Combustion of Hydrocarbons

$$\text{C}_3\text{H}_{8(g)} + 5\text{O}_{2(g)} \rightarrow 3\text{CO}_{2(g)} + 4\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

Inquiry Question 2: How are measurements made in chemistry?

The mole concept, mass-to-mole bridge networks, and limiting reagent boundary conditions.

1. Mass Definitions Matrix

To quantify macro-scale samples, atomic configurations are measured using the relative mass scale anchored to Carbon-12:

  • Relative Atomic Mass ($I_{\text{RAM}}$ or $A_r$): The weighted average mass of the naturally occurring isotopes of an element compared to $1/12\text{th}$ the mass of a single atom of Carbon-12.
  • Relative Molecular Mass ($M_r$): Applied strictly to covalent structures; the combined sum of the relative atomic masses of all component atoms indicated in the chemical molecular formula.
  • Relative Formula Mass: Applied to ionic crystalline lattices; the sum of atomic weights within the basic empirical unit formula (e.g., $\text{NaCl}$).

2. The Mole and Avogadro's Constant

Because individual atoms are impossibly small, chemists require a macroscopic counting unit to link microscopic counts with weighable reality.

Definition of the Mole ($n$): One mole is defined as the precise chemical amount containing exactly $6.022 \times 10^{23}$ elementary particles. This value is known as Avogadro's Constant ($N_A$).

3. The Mass-to-Mole Bridging Network

The primary core mathematical formula used to bridge macroscopic mass measurements ($m$, in grams) and chemical amount ($n$, in moles) is written as:

$$n = \frac{m}{\text{MM}}$$

Where:

  • $n$ = chemical amount in moles ($\text{mol}$)
  • $m$ = measured mass of the sample in grams ($\text{g}$)
  • $\text{MM}$ = molar mass of the chemical species in grams per mole ($\text{g}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$)

Stoichiometric Transformation Pathway Mapping

Follow this exact path for every mass-to-mass stoichiometry problem:

Mass of Substance A ($\text{g}$)
$\xrightarrow{\div \text{MM}_A}$
Moles of Substance A ($n$)
$\xrightarrow{\times \frac{\text{Coeff}_B}{\text{Coeff}_A}}$
Moles of Substance B ($n$)
$\xrightarrow{\times \text{MM}_B}$
Mass of Substance B ($\text{g}$)

4. Percentage Composition and Empirical Formula Derivations

Percentage Composition is the mass percentage of each element within a given compound, computed using the following equation:

$$\% \text{ Element} = \left( \frac{\text{Total Mass of Element in Formula}}{\text{Total Molar Mass of Compound}} \right) \times 100$$

Empirical Formula is the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms of each element present within a compound. It is calculated from gravimetric mass or percentage data using a systematic four-step process:

Step 1: Track Masses Step 2: Convert to Moles Step 3: Divide by Smallest Step 4: Scale to Whole Numbers
Assume a $100\text{g}$ mass sample if given percentages, converting $\%$ directly to grams. Divide each calculated mass component by its respective atomic mass: $n = \frac{m}{\text{MM}}$. Divide every single resulting mole value by the absolute lowest mole count obtained. If fractional numbers remain (e.g., $1.5$), multiply all values by a common factor to secure integers.

5. Limiting Reagents Matrix

In real-world reactions, reactants are rarely added in perfect stoichiometric ratios. The Limiting Reagent is the reactant that is entirely consumed first during a chemical process. It determines the maximum theoretical yield of all products. Reactants left over after the limiting species is entirely exhausted are called Excess Reagents.

Analytical Methodology: To determine which component is limiting, convert all starting reactant masses into moles. Divide each mole value by its corresponding stoichiometric coefficient from the balanced equation. The reactant with the absolute lowest calculated index is the limiting reagent.

Inquiry Question 3: How are chemicals in solutions measured?

Solution definitions, primary standards preparation, dilution equations, and concentration systems.

1. Mechanics of a Solution

A solution is a homogeneous mixture formed when a solute dissolves into a liquid medium called the solvent.

  • Ionic Solutes: Undergo structural dissociation. The strong electrostatic attractions within the ionic lattice are broken down by ion-dipole interactions with polar water molecules, surrounding the separated ions with hydration shells (e.g., $\text{NaCl}_{(s)} \rightarrow \text{Na}^+_{(aq)} + \text{Cl}^-_{(aq)}$).
  • Molecular Solutes: Undergo dissolution without breaking covalent bonds. The intermolecular forces holding separate molecules together are overcome by new intermolecular bonds formed with the solvent (e.g., sucrose or polar alcohols dissolving via hydrogen bonding).

2. Concentration Metrics Table

Chemists utilize diverse concentration tracking systems depending on the level of precision required across different fields:

Concentration Type Mathematical Formula Primary Laboratory Context
Molarity ($c$) $$c = \frac{n}{V} \quad (\text{mol}\cdot\text{L}^{-1})$$ High-precision chemical analysis and volumetric stoichiometry.
Grams per Litre $$\text{Conc} = \frac{m_{\text{solute}}}{V_{\text{solution}}} \quad (\text{g}\cdot\text{L}^{-1})$$ Standard laboratory reagent preparation.
Percentage Weight/Volume $$\% \text{ w/v} = \left( \frac{m_{\text{solute in g}}}{V_{\text{solution in mL}}} \right) \times 100$$ Commercial chemical formulations and pharmaceutical labeling.
Parts per Million (ppm) $$\text{ppm} = \frac{m_{\text{solute in mg}}}{V_{\text{solution in L}}} = \frac{\text{mg}}{\text{kg}}$$ Trace environmental analysis (e.g., heavy metal contaminants in waterways).

3. Volumetric Dilutions Theory

When a solvent is added to dilute a solution, the total volume increases and the overall concentration decreases, but the total number of moles of solute remains constant. This allows us to equate the initial and final chemical amounts ($n_1 = n_2$). Substituting $n = cV$ yields the standard dilution equation:

$$c_1V_1 = c_2V_2$$

Where:

  • $c_1, c_2$ = initial and final concentration values respectively
  • $V_1, V_2$ = initial and final solution volumes respectively (units must match on both sides)

4. Standard Solutions and Primary Standards

A standard solution is a solution of highly accurate, known concentration. Preparing one directly requires a chemical known as a primary standard. A primary standard must meet strict structural criteria to prevent measurement error:

  • Extreme Chemical Purity: Must contain zero unknown contaminants.
  • High Stability/Low Reactivity: Must not decompose or react with components in the air.
  • Non-Hygroscopic Nature: Must not absorb water weight from the surrounding atmosphere, ensuring mass measurements remain highly accurate.
  • Large Molar Mass: Minimises relative weighing errors on balances.

Examples: Anhydrous Sodium Carbonate ($\text{Na}_2\text{CO}_3$) and Potassium Hydrogen Phthalate ($\text{KHP}$) are excellent primary standards. Sodium Hydroxide ($\text{NaOH}$) is an unviable choice because it absorbs moisture and carbon dioxide from the air directly.

Inquiry Question 4: How does the Ideal Gas Law relate to all other Gas Laws?

Formal derivations, historical milestones, mathematical proofs, and unified ideal state formulations.

1. Individual Empirical Gas Laws

The behavior of gases is defined by four interdependent physical variables: Pressure ($P$), Volume ($V$), Temperature ($T$, which must always be evaluated in Kelvin: $K = ^\circ\text{C} + 273.15$), and Chemical Amount ($n$).

A. Boyle's Law (Pressure-Volume Relationship)

For a fixed mass of gas held at a constant temperature, the volume occupied by the gas is inversely proportional to its pressure. As pressure increases, the gas particles are forced closer together, decreasing the total volume:

$$V \propto \frac{1}{P} \quad \implies \quad PV = k_1 \quad \implies \quad P_1V_1 = P_2V_2$$

B. Charles' Law (Volume-Temperature Relationship)

For a fixed mass of gas held at a constant pressure, the volume is directly proportional to its absolute temperature. Heating a gas increases the kinetic energy of its particles, causing them to strike the container walls more forcefully. To maintain constant internal pressure, the gas must expand:

$$V \propto T \quad \implies \quad \frac{V}{T} = k_2 \quad \implies \quad \frac{V_1}{T_1} = \frac{V_2}{T_2}$$

C. Gay-Lussac's Law (Pressure-Temperature Relationship)

For a fixed mass of gas confined to a rigid, constant volume, the internal pressure is directly proportional to its absolute temperature. As kinetic energy increases, particles hit the rigid walls more frequently and with greater force, raising the overall pressure:

$$P \propto T \quad \implies \quad \frac{P}{T} = k_3 \quad \implies \quad \frac{P_1}{T_1} = \frac{P_2}{T_2}$$

D. Avogadro's Law (Volume-Mole Relationship)

At a constant temperature and pressure, equal volumes of all gases contain an identical number of chemical moles. Volume scales linearly with the number of gas particles present:

$$V \propto n \quad \implies \quad \frac{V}{n} = k_4 \quad \implies \quad \frac{V_1}{n_1} = \frac{V_2}{n_2}$$
Gay-Lussac’s Law of Combining Volumes: When measured at constant temperature and pressure, the gaseous volumes consumed or produced in a chemical reaction combine in simple, whole-number ratios that match their stoichiometric coefficients.

2. Formal Derivation of the Ideal Gas Equation

The individual empirical relationships discovered historically can be integrated into a single, comprehensive law. We can summarize our four proportionalities as follows:

From Boyle's Law: $V \propto \frac{1}{P}$

From Charles' Law: $V \propto T$

From Avogadro's Law: $V \propto n$

Combining these independent proportional variables into a single joint variation expression yields:

$$V \propto \frac{n \cdot T}{P}$$

To convert this proportionality into an absolute equation, we introduce a universal constant of proportionality, defined as the Universal Gas Constant ($R$):

$$V = R \left( \frac{nT}{P} \right)$$

Rearranging this expression algebraically gives us the final Ideal Gas Equation:

$$PV = nRT$$

Where standard SI units must be strictly aligned to match the constant $R = 8.314 \text{ J}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}\cdot\text{K}^{-1}$:

  • $P$ = Absolute Pressure in Kilopascals ($\text{kPa}$) or Pascals ($\text{Pa}$)
  • $V$ = Volume in Litres ($\text{L}$) or Cubic Metres ($\text{m}^3$)
  • $n$ = Chemical Amount in Moles ($\text{mol}$)
  • $R$ = Universal Gas Constant ($8.314 \text{ U}$)
  • $T$ = Temperature in Kelvin ($\text{K} = ^\circ\text{C} + 273.15$)

3. Molar Volume of a Gas ($V_M$)

Because equal moles of any gas occupy an identical volume under the same physical conditions, we can isolate the volume of exactly one mole of gas ($n = 1$) at standard reference points using the Ideal Gas Equation ($V = \frac{nRT}{P}$):

  • At SLC (Standard Laboratory Conditions): $T = 25^\circ\text{C} = 298.15\text{K}$, $P = 100\text{kPa}$. Substituting these parameters yields: $$V = \frac{1 \times 8.314 \times 298.15}{100} = 24.79 \text{ L}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$$
  • At STP (Standard Temperature and Pressure): $T = 0^\circ\text{C} = 273.15\text{K}$, $P = 100\text{kPa}$. Substituting these parameters yields: $$V = \frac{1 \times 8.314 \times 273.15}{100} = 22.71 \text{ L}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$$
