Question 1 (3 Marks)Foundational Core

Module 1: Gallium has two naturally occurring isotopes: $^{69}\text{Ga}$ (mass $68.926\text{ u}$) and $^{71}\text{Ga}$ (mass $70.925\text{ u}$). If the relative atomic mass of Gallium is $69.723\text{ u}$, calculate the percentage abundance of each isotope to two decimal places.

Exemplar Worked Solution

Let $x$ be the fractional abundance of $^{69}\text{Ga}$ and $(1-x)$ be the fractional abundance of $^{71}\text{Ga}$.

$$68.926x + 70.925(1 - x) = 69.723$$ $$68.926x + 70.925 - 70.925x = 69.723$$ $$-1.999x = -1.202 \implies x = 0.6013$$

Therefore, $^{69}\text{Ga}$ is $60.13\%$ and $^{71}\text{Ga}$ is $39.87\%$.

Question 2 (3 Marks)Intermediate Analytical

Module 1: Explain why the first ionisation energy of Oxygen is anomalously slightly lower than that of Nitrogen, despite being further right across Period 2.

Exemplar Worked Solution

Nitrogen has an electron configuration of $1s^2 2s^2 2p^3$, meaning its 2p subshell is exactly half-full with three unpaired electrons, providing extra stability. Oxygen has an electron configuration of $1s^2 2s^2 2p^4$. The fourth electron must pair up with another electron in one of the 2p orbitals. This pairing creates electrostatic repulsion between the two electrons in the same orbital, making it slightly easier (requiring less energy) to remove one of these paired electrons compared to an unpaired electron in Nitrogen.

Question 3 (4 Marks)Advanced Mastery

Module 1: Contrast the electrical conductivity and melting point of solid Sodium Chloride ($\text{NaCl}$) with that of solid Silicon Dioxide ($\text{SiO}_2$). Explain these differences with specific reference to their bonding architectures.

Exemplar Worked Solution

Electrical Conductivity: Both $\text{NaCl}_{(s)}$ and $\text{SiO}_{2(s)}$ are electrical insulators in the solid state. In $\text{NaCl}$, the ions ($\text{Na}^+$ and $\text{Cl}^-$) are fixed rigidly in an ionic lattice by strong electrostatic forces, meaning there are no mobile charge carriers. In $\text{SiO}_2$, all valence electrons are localised in strong covalent bonds within a continuous 3D network, leaving no delocalised electrons to carry charge.

Melting Point: Both possess high melting points, but $\text{SiO}_2$ is significantly higher. Melting $\text{NaCl}$ requires overcoming the strong electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions. Melting $\text{SiO}_2$ requires breaking extremely strong intramolecular covalent bonds extending continuously throughout the giant network, which demands vastly more thermal energy.

Question 4 (3 Marks)Foundational Core

Module 2: What volume of exactly $0.150\text{ mol}\cdot\text{L}^{-1}\text{ H}_2\text{SO}_4$ is required to completely neutralise $25.0\text{ mL}$ of $0.200\text{ mol}\cdot\text{L}^{-1}\text{ NaOH}$ solution?

Exemplar Worked Solution

Balanced Equation: $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_{4(aq)} + 2\text{NaOH}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Na}_2\text{SO}_{4(aq)} + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$

$$n(\text{NaOH}) = c \times V = 0.200 \times 0.0250 = 0.00500\text{ mol}$$

From the $1:2$ stoichiometric ratio, $n(\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4)$ required $= 0.00500 / 2 = 0.00250\text{ mol}$.

$$V(\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4) = \frac{n}{c} = \frac{0.00250}{0.150} = 0.01666...\text{ L} = 16.7\text{ mL}$$

Applying the 3 significant figure rule from initial data: $16.7\text{ mL}$.

Question 5 (4 Marks)Advanced Mastery

Module 2: A compound consists of $40.0\%$ Carbon, $6.71\%$ Hydrogen, and $53.29\%$ Oxygen by mass. If a $2.50\text{ g}$ sample of the vaporised compound occupies $0.621\text{ L}$ at $120^\circ\text{C}$ and $105\text{ kPa}$, determine its empirical and molecular formula.

Exemplar Worked Solution

1. Empirical Formula: Assume $100\text{ g}$.
$n(\text{C}) = 40.0 / 12.01 = 3.33$
$n(\text{H}) = 6.71 / 1.008 = 6.66$
$n(\text{O}) = 53.29 / 16.00 = 3.33$
Divide by $3.33$: Ratio is $1:2:1$. Empirical Formula = $\text{CH}_2\text{O}$ (Empirical Mass $\approx 30.03\text{ g}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$).

2. Molecular Mass: Use $PV = nRT \implies M = \frac{mRT}{PV}$.
$T = 120 + 273.15 = 393.15\text{ K}$.
$$M = \frac{2.50 \times 8.314 \times 393.15}{105 \times 0.621} = \frac{8171.62}{65.205} = 125.32\text{ g}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$$

Scale Factor: $125.32 / 30.03 \approx 4.17$. Due to experimental error tracking in such questions, the closest integer ratio multiplier is 4 (assuming ideal gas limits or slight deviations). If purely mathematical based on the prompt values: a multiplier of 4 yields $\text{C}_4\text{H}_8\text{O}_4$ ($120.1\text{ g}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$).

Question 6 (5 Marks)Legend Tier

Module 2 & 3 Bridge: Exactly $15.0\text{ g}$ of pure Aluminium powder is mixed into $200.0\text{ mL}$ of $1.50\text{ mol}\cdot\text{L}^{-1}$ Copper(II) Sulfate solution. Calculate the final dry mass of solid Copper precipitated and the concentration of Aluminium ions left in the solution. Assume no volume change.

Exemplar Worked Solution

Equation: $2\text{Al}_{(s)} + 3\text{CuSO}_{4(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Al}_2(\text{SO}_4)_{3(aq)} + 3\text{Cu}_{(s)}$

Initial moles:
$n(\text{Al}) = 15.0 / 26.98 = 0.556\text{ mol}$
$n(\text{CuSO}_4) = 1.50 \times 0.2000 = 0.300\text{ mol}$

Determine Limiting Reagent:
Ratio $\text{Al}:\text{CuSO}_4$ is $2:3$. For $0.300\text{ mol CuSO}_4$, we need $0.300 \times \frac{2}{3} = 0.200\text{ mol Al}$. Since we have $0.556\text{ mol Al}$, $\text{CuSO}_4$ is the limiting reagent.

Product Calculation:
$n(\text{Cu}) = n(\text{CuSO}_4) = 0.300\text{ mol}$.
Mass $\text{Cu} = 0.300 \times 63.55 = 19.065 \implies \mathbf{19.1\text{ g}}$ (3 s.f.).

Aluminium Ion Concentration:
The moles of $\text{Al}_2(\text{SO}_4)_3$ formed $= 0.300 / 3 = 0.100\text{ mol}$.
Each mole of $\text{Al}_2(\text{SO}_4)_3$ yields 2 moles of $\text{Al}^{3+}$ ions $\implies 0.200\text{ mol Al}^{3+}$.
$[\text{Al}^{3+}] = 0.200\text{ mol} / 0.2000\text{ L} = \mathbf{1.00\text{ mol}\cdot\text{L}^{-1}}$.

Question 7 (3 Marks)Foundational Core

Module 3: State the oxidation number of the bolded element in the following species: (a) $\mathbf{Cr}_2\text{O}_7^{2-}$, (b) $\text{Na}\mathbf{H}$, (c) $\text{H}_2\mathbf{O}_2$.

Exemplar Worked Solution

(a) $\text{Cr}_2\text{O}_7^{2-}$: $2x + 7(-2) = -2 \implies 2x = +12 \implies \mathbf{Cr = +6}$.

(b) $\text{NaH}$: Sodium is a Group 1 metal (always +1 in compounds), so Hydrogen acts as a hydride ion. $\mathbf{H = -1}$.

(c) $\text{H}_2\text{O}_2$: In peroxides, oxygen is an exception to the -2 rule. Since $H = +1$, $2(+1) + 2x = 0 \implies \mathbf{O = -1}$.

Question 9 (3 Marks)Intermediate Analytical

Module 3: State three factors that can increase the rate of a chemical reaction. For each, clearly state *why* it increases the rate according to Collision Theory.

Exemplar Worked Solution

1. Increasing Temperature: Increases the average kinetic energy of the particles. A significantly larger fraction of collisions now possess energy greater than the activation energy ($E \ge E_a$), exponentially increasing the frequency of *successful* collisions.

2. Increasing Concentration/Pressure: Crowds more reactant particles into a given volume, which proportionally increases the *total* frequency of collisions per unit time.

3. Adding a Catalyst: Provides an alternative reaction mechanism with a lower activation energy ($E_a$). This allows a larger proportion of existing collisions to be successful without requiring extra thermal energy.

Question 10 (4 Marks)Advanced Mastery

Module 4: Below is an unlabelled Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution grid. Using HTML/CSS representation, sketch how the distribution curve shifts when the temperature is significantly increased ($T_2 > T_1$), and explain how this visual shift mathematically relates to the reaction rate.

Exemplar Worked Solution

Explanation: At higher temperature $T_2$, the peak of the curve flattens and shifts to the right. While the total area under the curve (total particles) remains identical, the area under the curve *to the right* of the Activation Energy ($E_a$) line is significantly larger. This visualizes a mathematical increase in the specific fraction of molecules possessing $E_k \ge E_a$, vastly accelerating the reaction rate.

Question 11 (2 Marks)Foundational Core

Module 4: A $50.0\text{ g}$ sample of liquid water absorbs $2.50\text{ kJ}$ of heat energy. Assuming no phase change occurs, calculate the final temperature of the water if its initial temperature was $22.0^\circ\text{C}$ (Specific heat capacity $c = 4.184\text{ J}\cdot\text{g}^{-1}\cdot\text{K}^{-1}$).

Exemplar Worked Solution

Convert heat to Joules: $q = 2500\text{ J}$.

$$q = m c \Delta T \implies \Delta T = \frac{q}{m c}$$ $$\Delta T = \frac{2500}{50.0 \times 4.184} = 11.95^\circ\text{C}$$ $$T_{\text{final}} = T_{\text{initial}} + \Delta T = 22.0 + 11.95 = \mathbf{34.0^\circ\text{C}}$$ (3 s.f.).
Question 12 (3 Marks)Intermediate Analytical

Module 4: Determine the enthalpy of reaction ($\Delta H^\circ$) for the process $2\text{C}_{(s)} + \text{H}_{2(g)} \rightarrow \text{C}_2\text{H}_{2(g)}$ given the following combustion data:

(1) $\text{C}_{(s)} + \text{O}_{2(g)} \rightarrow \text{CO}_{2(g)} \quad \Delta H = -393.5\text{ kJ}$
(2) $\text{H}_{2(g)} + 0.5\text{O}_{2(g)} \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)} \quad \Delta H = -285.8\text{ kJ}$
(3) $\text{C}_2\text{H}_{2(g)} + 2.5\text{O}_{2(g)} \rightarrow 2\text{CO}_{2(g)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)} \quad \Delta H = -1299.6\text{ kJ}$

Exemplar Worked Solution

Target: $2\text{C}_{(s)} + \text{H}_{2(g)} \rightarrow \text{C}_2\text{H}_{2(g)}$

  • Eq (1) $\times 2$: $2\text{C} + 2\text{O}_2 \rightarrow 2\text{CO}_2 \quad (\Delta H = 2 \times -393.5 = -787.0\text{ kJ})$
  • Eq (2) kept as is: $\text{H}_2 + 0.5\text{O}_2 \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{O} \quad (\Delta H = -285.8\text{ kJ})$
  • Eq (3) reversed: $2\text{CO}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{C}_2\text{H}_2 + 2.5\text{O}_2 \quad (\Delta H = +1299.6\text{ kJ})$

Summing the enthalpies: $-787.0 - 285.8 + 1299.6 = \mathbf{+226.8\text{ kJ\cdot mol^{-1}}}$.

Question 13 (2 Marks)Foundational Core

Module 4: Without performing any calculations, predict the sign of the entropy change ($\Delta S$) for the Haber Process: $\text{N}_{2(g)} + 3\text{H}_{2(g)} \rightleftharpoons 2\text{NH}_{3(g)}$, and briefly explain your reasoning.

Exemplar Worked Solution

The sign of $\Delta S$ is negative. The reaction converts 4 moles of disordered gaseous reactants ($1\text{ N}_2 + 3\text{ H}_2$) into only 2 moles of gaseous products. A decrease in the number of gaseous molecules reduces the total number of accessible microstates and positional randomness, constituting a decrease in overall entropy.

Question 14 (4 Marks)Advanced Mastery

Module 4: For a given chemical reaction, $\Delta H = +85.0\text{ kJ\cdot mol^{-1}}$ and $\Delta S = +125\text{ J\cdot K^{-1} mol^{-1}}$. Calculate the precise temperature (in $^\circ\text{C}$) at which the system crosses over from being non-spontaneous to spontaneous.

Exemplar Worked Solution

At the spontaneity threshold, Gibbs Free Energy $\Delta G = 0$.

$$\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S = 0 \implies T = \frac{\Delta H}{\Delta S}$$

Convert $\Delta S$ into matching units: $+125\text{ J} / 1000 = 0.125\text{ kJ\cdot K^{-1} mol^{-1}}$.

$$T = \frac{85.0}{0.125} = 680\text{ K}$$

Convert to Celsius: $T(^\circ\text{C}) = 680 - 273.15 = \mathbf{406.85^\circ\text{C}}$. (Reaction is spontaneous at temperatures strictly above this).

Question 15 (5 Marks)Legend Tier

Cross-Module Integration (Mods 2 & 4): A $10.0\text{ g}$ sample of Ammonium Nitrate ($\text{NH}_4\text{NO}_3$, $M = 80.04\text{ g\cdot mol^{-1}}$) dissolves completely in $150.0\text{ mL}$ of water originally at $25.0^\circ\text{C}$ in a coffee-cup calorimeter. If the standard molar enthalpy of dissolution for $\text{NH}_4\text{NO}_3$ is $+25.7\text{ kJ\cdot mol^{-1}}$, calculate the final lowest temperature the solution matrix drops to.

Exemplar Worked Solution

1. Calculate moles of solute:
$n(\text{NH}_4\text{NO}_3) = 10.0 / 80.04 = 0.1249\text{ mol}$.

2. Calculate total heat absorbed by the reaction ($q_{\text{rxn}}$):
$q_{\text{rxn}} = n \times \Delta H = 0.1249 \times 25.7\text{ kJ} = +3.211\text{ kJ} = 3211\text{ J}$.
Since the reaction is endothermic, this thermal energy is stripped *away* from the surrounding water layer.

3. Calculate the resulting temperature drop of the water matrix:
$q_{\text{water}} = -3211\text{ J}$. (Assume the mass of the solvent matrix is roughly $150.0\text{ g}$ of water, though rigorous texts might use total mass $160.0\text{ g}$; we will use standard NESA assumption $m = 150.0\text{ g}$ solvent, or $160.0\text{ g}$ system. Let's use $150.0\text{ g}$ to represent pure solvent cooling).
$q = m c \Delta T \implies \Delta T = \frac{-3211}{150.0 \times 4.184} = -5.12\text{ K}$.

4. Final Temperature:
$T_{\text{final}} = 25.0 - 5.12 = \mathbf{19.9^\circ\text{C}}$ (3 s.f.).

Question 16 (3 Marks)Intermediate Analytical

Module 1 & 3 Bridge: Contrast the processes of atomic alpha decay ($\alpha$) and beta decay ($\beta$) in terms of their physical particle emissions and how they structurally alter the parent nucleus's atomic mass and number.

Exemplar Worked Solution

Alpha Decay: Emits an alpha particle (a helium nucleus, $^4_2\text{He}$), consisting of 2 protons and 2 neutrons. This process decreases the parent's atomic mass number by exactly 4 and its atomic number by 2, creating a new element shifted two places left on the periodic table.

Beta Decay: Emits a high-velocity electron ($^0_{-1}e$). This occurs when a neutron internally fragments into a proton and an electron. Because a neutron becomes a proton, the total mass number remains unchanged (0 shift), but the atomic number increases by 1, shifting the element one place to the right on the periodic table.

Question 17 (3 Marks)Foundational Core

Module 2: A sealed syringe traps $250.0\text{ mL}$ of an ideal gas at $125\text{ kPa}$ and $300\text{ K}$. If the syringe is compressed to $100.0\text{ mL}$ while the temperature is heated to $450\text{ K}$, calculate the new internal pressure of the system.

Question 19 (3 Marks)Intermediate Analytical

Module 4: Why does the experimental molar heat of combustion of an alcohol, determined via school laboratory calorimetry, continually fall below the accepted theoretical literature value?

Exemplar Worked Solution

1. Thermal Dissipation: The primary error is massive heat loss from the open flame to the surrounding ambient air and the absorption of heat by the physical metal/glass calorimeter container, bypassing the water body. This lowers the measured $\Delta T$.

2. Incomplete Combustion: Standard spirit burners often lack sufficient oxygen access to complete full $\text{CO}_2$ conversions. The presence of black carbon soot indicates incomplete combustion, which intrinsically releases far less thermal energy per mole of fuel.

Question 20 (4 Marks)Advanced Mastery

Module 1 & 4 Bridge: Explain how the macroscopic structure and intermolecular forces within pure liquid water ($\text{H}_2\text{O}$) heavily dictate its unusually high specific heat capacity compared to identically sized non-polar molecules like Methane ($\text{CH}_4$).

Exemplar Worked Solution

Because Oxygen is highly electronegative, water molecules form extremely strong, permanent hydrogen bonds between the partially positive Hydrogen poles and the partially negative Oxygen poles of adjacent molecules. Methane, being perfectly non-polar, only possesses weak momentary London dispersion forces.

Temperature is a measure of translational kinetic energy (movement). Before water molecules can move faster and increase their macroscopic temperature, massive amounts of incoming thermal energy must first be consumed to disrupt and overcome this rigid hydrogen-bonding network. This energy sink gives water an exceptionally high specific heat capacity ($\approx 4.18\text{ J\cdot g^{-1}\cdot K^{-1}}$).

Question 25 (6 Marks)Final Legend Tier Assessment

Comprehensive Capstone Synthesis: A student tests an unknown, highly toxic biological alkaloid compound found in a plant. It dissolves instantly in water, establishing its strong polar nature. A mass spectrometer analysis yields a fractional makeup of $70.58\%\text{ C}$, $5.92\%\text{ H}$, and $23.50\%\text{ O}$ by mass. The molecular mass is later verified via ideal gas constraints to be identically double its empirical form. When the pure sample is combusted violently inside a sealed oxygen bomb calorimeter, the temperature of the internal casing spikes wildly (producing $\Delta H = -4200\text{ kJ\cdot mol^{-1}}$). At incredibly high combustion temperatures ($T > 1800\text{ K}$), the specific oxidation pathways shift, driving the net system entropy negative ($\Delta S < 0$).

Provide a comprehensive analysis deducing:
1) The true molecular formula of the alkaloid.
2) A thermodynamic justification for exactly why the combustion reaction could suddenly shift from spontaneous to non-spontaneous at extreme temperature peaks.

Legend Exemplar Worked Solution

1. Structural Formula Matrix:
Assume $100\text{ g}$ of the alkaloid.
$n(\text{C}) = 70.58 / 12.01 = 5.877\text{ mol}$
$n(\text{H}) = 5.92 / 1.008 = 5.873\text{ mol}$
$n(\text{O}) = 23.50 / 16.00 = 1.469\text{ mol}$
Normalise by dividing by the lowest value ($1.469$):
$\text{C} = 4, \text{H} = 4, \text{O} = 1$. The Empirical Formula is $\text{C}_4\text{H}_4\text{O}$.
Since the prompting explicit criteria declares the molecular mass is identically double its empirical frame, multiply coefficients by 2:
Molecular Formula = $\text{C}_8\text{H}_8\text{O}_2$.

2. Thermodynamic Edge-Case Spontaneity ($\Delta G$):
Standard combustion reactions are wildly exothermic ($\Delta H < 0$). The problem notes that at specific high temperatures, an alternate oxidation pathway forces entropy into the negative ($\Delta S < 0$), likely converting heavy gaseous chains into rigid condensed oxides.
Gibbs Equation: $\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S$.
Since $\Delta S$ is negative, the term $-T\Delta S$ becomes a massively positive additive integer. At sufficiently extreme temperatures ($T > 1800\text{ K}$), the absolute value of the positive $T\Delta S$ barrier overrides the exothermic enthalpy push ($T\Delta S > |\Delta H|$). This drives the final Gibbs Free Energy above zero ($\Delta G > 0$), slamming the reaction into a non-spontaneous thermodynamic lockout state.
