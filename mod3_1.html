EXEMPLAR 1 Foundational Baseline

Gravimetric Yield and Solubility Metathesis

A $50.0\text{ mL}$ aliquot of $0.200\text{ mol}\cdot\text{L}^{-1}$ barium chloride ($\text{BaCl}_2$) solution is reacted with an excess of sodium sulfate ($\text{Na}_2\text{SO}_4$) solution. Deduce the macroscopic indicators of this interaction, provide the balanced net ionic expression, and isolate the final dry mass of precipitate generated.

Exemplar Worked Solution & Response Parameters:

1. Macroscopic Observations: Upon introducing the clear, colourless sodium sulfate solution to the clear, colourless barium chloride solution, a fine, opaque white precipitate immediately forms throughout the chemical solution, settling out of the matrix over time.

2. Molecular and Net Ionic Formulations:

Full Molecular Equation:
$$\text{BaCl}_{2(aq)} + \text{Na}_2\text{SO}_{4(aq)} \rightarrow \text{BaSO}_{4(s)}\downarrow + 2\text{NaCl}_{(aq)}$$

Net Ionic Equation (omitting spectator chloride and sodium ions):
$$\text{Ba}^{2+}_{(aq)} + \text{SO}_{4(aq)}^{2-} \rightarrow \text{BaSO}_{4(s)}$$

3. Quantitative Gravimetric Sequence:

  • Calculate the chemical amount of the limiting barium ions: $$n(\text{Ba}^{2+}) = c \times V = 0.200\text{ mol}\cdot\text{L}^{-1} \times 0.0500\text{ L} = 0.0100\text{ mol}$$
  • Determine molar mass of the insoluble precipitate lattice ($\text{BaSO}_4$): $$\text{MM}(\text{BaSO}_4) = 137.3 + 32.07 + (4 \times 16.00) = 233.37\text{ g}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$$
  • Apply the stoichiometric conversion factor ($1:1$ molar ratio): $$m(\text{BaSO}_4) = n \times \text{MM} = 0.0100\text{ mol} \times 233.37\text{ g}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1} = 2.3337\text{ g}$$
  • Apply Significant Figure Ceilings: Both values ($50.0\text{ mL}$ and $0.200\text{ M}$) contain three significant figures.

Final Solution Mass: $2.33\text{ g}$

EXEMPLAR 2 Syllabus Proficient

Advanced Thermodynamic Heat Capacity Profiles

A student builds a non-insulated laboratory calorimeter to determine the molar enthalpy of combustion ($\Delta H_c$) of an unknown liquid fuel, methanol ($\text{CH}_3\text{OH}$, $\text{MM} = 32.042\text{ g}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$). Burning $1.62\text{ g}$ of the liquid fuel under a flask containing $250.0\text{ g}$ of pristine water causes a temperature shift from $22.1^\circ\text{C}$ to $48.7^\circ\text{C}$. Calculate the raw molar enthalpy change, identify structural errors that explain any divergence from the accepted theoretical value ($-726\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$), and determine the final value using correct significant figures.

Exemplar Worked Solution & Response Parameters:

1. Quantitative Thermodynamic Calculations:

  • Isolate system temperature displacement: $$\Delta T = T_{\text{final}} - T_{\text{initial}} = 48.7^\circ\text{C} - 22.1^\circ\text{C} = 26.6\text{ K}$$
  • Determine thermal energy absorbed by the water layer ($q = m c \Delta T$): $$q = 250.0\text{ g} \times 4.184\text{ J}\cdot\text{g}^{-1}\cdot\text{K}^{-1} \times 26.6\text{ K} = 27823.6\text{ J} = 27.8236\text{ kJ}$$
  • Determine the moles of methanol fuel oxidised: $$n = \frac{m}{\text{MM}} = \frac{1.62\text{ g}}{32.042\text{ g}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}} = 0.0505586\text{ mol}$$
  • Determine molar enthalpy ($\Delta H_c$): $$\Delta H_c = -\frac{q}{n} = -\frac{27.8236\text{ kJ}}{0.0505586\text{ mol}} = -550.323\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$$
  • Apply Precision Controls: The values $1.62\text{ g}$ and $26.6\text{ K}$ limit the precision to 3 significant figures.

Calculated Value: $-550\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$

2. Critical Validity Analysis: The experimental value ($-550\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$) is lower than the standard theoretical value ($-726\text{ kJ}\cdot\text{mol}^{-1}$). Significant heat loss occurred to the surrounding atmosphere and the glass beaker itself because the open calorimeter setup lacked insulation, a draft shield, or a lid. This caused the measured $\Delta T$ to be lower than expected, resulting in an underestimated enthalpy calculation.

EXEMPLAR 4 Syllabus Proficient

Indigenous Poison Detoxification Kinetics

Analyse how Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Peoples use chemical principles to safely process the highly poisonous seeds of the cycad palm (Cycas media). Detail the key chemical species involved and explain the specific mechanisms that make the seeds safe to eat.

Exemplar Worked Solution & Response Parameters:

1. Identification of Toxic Hazard: Raw cycad seeds contain hazardous levels of the water-soluble neurotoxin and carcinogen known as cycasin.

2. Thermodynamic and Dissolution Mechanisms:

  • Thermal Alteration (Roasting): Roasting the seeds in open pits transfers thermal energy into the material, which breaks down the tough outer hulls and helps denature volatile toxic compounds.
  • Leaching (Solubility & Diffusion Kinetics): Slicing the roasted seeds increases their total surface area, maximizing exposure to the solvent. The seeds are then placed in woven grass baskets and submerged in flowing creek waters. Because cycasin is a highly polar molecule, it dissolves readily in water due to favorable ion-dipole and hydrogen-bonding interactions.
  • Equilibrium Dynamic: Submerging the seeds in flowing water maintains a steep concentration gradient between the internal seed tissue and the surrounding water. The moving water continuously carries away the dissolved cycasin, preventing the system from reaching equilibrium and driving the dynamic process until the seeds are completely detoxified.
EXEMPLAR 5 Selective / Extension Tier

Activation Barriers and Kinetic Catalysis Mechanics

Explain how adding an external manganese dioxide ($\text{MnO}_2$) catalyst alters the decomposition rate of hydrogen peroxide ($2\text{H}_2\text{O}_{2(aq)} \rightarrow 2\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)} + \text{O}_{2(g)}$). Use collision theory principles and a Maxwell-Boltzmann energy distribution framework to support your explanation.

Exemplar Worked Solution & Response Parameters:

1. Collision Theory Framework: Uncatalyzed hydrogen peroxide molecules decompose slowly at room temperature because the reaction has a high activation energy ($E_a$), meaning few ambient molecular collisions possess enough energy to overcome the reaction barrier.

2. Catalytic Mechanism: The solid manganese dioxide ($\text{MnO}_2$) catalyst provides an alternative heterogeneous physical surface for the reaction. This alternative pathway has a significantly lower activation energy ($E_a$). The catalyst participates in the intermediate steps but is completely regenerated at the end of the process.

3. Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution Dynamics: Lowering the activation energy from $E_{a,\text{uncatalyzed}}$ to $E_{a,\text{catalyzed}}$ changes the energy requirements for the system. A significantly larger fraction of the reactant particles now possess kinetic energy equal to or greater than the new activation energy threshold ($E_k \ge E_{a,\text{catalyzed}}$). As a result, the frequency of successful molecular collisions per unit of time increases, accelerating the macroscopic reaction rate and causing rapid oxygen gas evolution.
